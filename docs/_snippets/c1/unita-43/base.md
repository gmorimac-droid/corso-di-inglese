# Modal Verbs (Speculation & Deduction)

## Obiettivo della lezione

In questa unità impari a:

- fare deduzioni logiche sul **presente** e sul **passato**
- esprimere diversi gradi di certezza usando **must, may, might, could, can’t**
- distinguere tra **fatto** e **interpretazione**
- usare correttamente le strutture **modal + base verb** e **modal + have + past participle**
- descrivere situazioni in modo più maturo e naturale

---

## 1. Perché questa unità è importante

A livelli più alti non basta più dire solo **what happened**. Devi anche saper dire:

- cosa **pensi** che stia succedendo
- cosa **deduci** da certi segnali
- quanto sei **sicuro** della tua interpretazione

Per questo i modal verbs di deduzione sono fondamentali: ti permettono di passare dalla semplice descrizione alla **lettura della realtà**.

---

## 2. Fatti vs interpretazioni

Confronta:

- The office is empty. → **fatto osservabile**
- They must be in a meeting. → **deduzione**

Oppure:

- He didn’t answer the phone. → **fatto**
- He might be busy. → **interpretazione**

Questa distinzione è il cuore della lezione.

---

## 3. Deduction nel presente

### Must

Usiamo **must** quando la deduzione è molto forte.

- She must be tired.
- They must be at work.
- This must be the right address.

### May / Might / Could

Usiamo **may, might, could** quando qualcosa è possibile ma non certa.

- He may be at home.
- She might be working.
- They could be on the train.

### Can’t

Usiamo **can’t** quando riteniamo qualcosa impossibile.

- He can’t be at home. His car isn’t there.
- This can’t be true.
- They can’t be ready yet.

---

## 4. Grado di certezza

| Struttura | Significato |
|---|---|
| must | quasi certo |
| may / might / could | possibile |
| can’t | impossibile |

Non stiamo parlando di regole o permessi. Qui i modali servono a esprimere **inference**, cioè deduzione.

---

## 5. Deduction nel passato

Quando vogliamo fare deduzioni su qualcosa che probabilmente è successo nel passato, usiamo:

`modal + have + past participle`

### Must have + past participle

- He must have left early.
- She must have forgotten the meeting.
- They must have taken the wrong train.

### May / Might / Could have + past participle

- He might have missed the bus.
- She may have gone home already.
- They could have misunderstood the instructions.

### Can’t have + past participle

- He can’t have done it alone.
- She can’t have seen the email.
- They can’t have arrived yet.

---

## 6. Presente o passato?

Confronta:

- He must be tired. → deduzione sul **presente**
- He must have been tired. → deduzione sul **passato**

- She might be at home. → forse è a casa **adesso**
- She might have gone home. → forse è andata a casa **prima**

La struttura cambia in base al **momento** a cui fai riferimento.

---

## 7. Mini dialoghi

### Dialogue 1

**A:** The lights are off and nobody is answering.
**B:** They must be out.
**A:** Or they might be upstairs.

### Dialogue 2

**A:** Tom wasn’t at the meeting this morning.
**B:** He might have forgotten about it.
**A:** No, he can’t have forgotten. He sent the agenda yesterday.

### Dialogue 3

**A:** Maria looks exhausted.
**B:** She must have worked late last night.

---

## 8. Errori tipici da evitare

### Errore 1: aggiungere *to* dopo il modal

❌ He must to be tired.  
✅ He must be tired.

### Errore 2: sbagliare la struttura del passato

❌ He must has gone home.  
✅ He must have gone home.

### Errore 3: usare *can’t have* come se fosse presente

❌ He can’t have at home.  
✅ He can’t be at home.  
✅ He can’t have gone home yet.

### Errore 4: confondere fatto e deduzione

❌ He is late, so he is in traffic.  
✅ He is late, so he **must be** stuck in traffic.

## 9. Controlled practice

### A. Complete the sentences

1. He isn’t answering. He ___ be busy.
2. She isn’t here. She ___ have left already.
3. They ___ be in the office. The building is closed.
4. He looks worried. He ___ have received bad news.

### B. Choose the best option

1. The lights are on. They **must / can’t** be at home.
2. She missed the train. She **must have / must be** left late.
3. He isn’t online. He **might be / might have** asleep.
4. They know the answer. They **can’t have / must** understood the question.

### C. Rewrite as a deduction

1. Maybe she is at work.  
2. I’m sure he forgot.  
3. It’s impossible that they are ready.  
4. Maybe she missed the email.

---

## 10. Speaking task

Look at a situation and make deductions.

Possible prompts:

- a person who looks tired
- an empty classroom
- a colleague who didn’t reply
- a friend who arrived late
- a cancelled event

Try to use:

- **must**
- **might / may / could**
- **can’t**
- at least one deduction about the **past**

---

## 11. Writing task

Write **10 original sentences**.

Include:

- 3 deductions about the present
- 3 deductions about the past
- 2 sentences with **can’t**
- 2 sentences with **might / may / could**

Then write a short paragraph describing a mysterious situation using at least **five** modal deductions.

---

## 12. Final checklist

You are ready to move on if you can:

- distinguish clearly between **fact** and **interpretation**
- use **must, might, may, could, can’t** for present deduction
- use **must have, might have, could have, can’t have** for past deduction
- choose structures according to the correct **time reference**
- avoid basic structural errors with modal verbs
