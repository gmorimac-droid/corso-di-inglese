    Chunk 01 core everyday

    Chunk 1 — Core Everyday Vocabulary

    Questo primo chunk raccoglie il lessico quotidiano fondamentale del corso, con una progressione che parte da A1-A2 ma si espande fino a B1-B2 attraverso collocations, usage notes e blocchi Professional.
    L'obiettivo non è solo conoscere più parole, ma imparare a usare parole naturali in combinazioni naturali.

    1. Personal Identity and Basic Personal Information

    Core vocabulary

    • name
    • surname / last name
    • first name
    • age
    • date of birth
    • nationality
    • country
    • city
    • address
    • phone number
    • email address
    • job
    • student
    • language
    • beginner
    • intermediate
    • advanced

    Useful combinations

    • introduce yourself
    • spell your name
    • say your surname
    • give your email address
    • tell someone your age
    • mention your nationality
    • speak English
    • study languages
    • work as a teacher
    • live in Rome

    Typical sentence patterns

    • My name is ...
    • I'm from ...
    • I live in ...
    • I'm ... years old.
    • I work as a ...
    • I study ...
    • I speak Italian and English.

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • I am 25 years → I am 25 years old
    • I live at Rome → I live in Rome
    • I work like engineer → I work as an engineer
    • I am married with → I am married to
    • I know to speak English → I can speak English / I know English

    Mini examples

    • Hi, my name is Laura and I'm from Milan.
    • I work as a project manager and I speak English every day.
    • Could you spell your surname, please?

    Professional upgrade

    At higher levels, personal information becomes more nuanced: - background - profile - professional background - area of expertise - field - sector - role - position - responsibilities - qualifications

    Higher-value expressions

    • I work in the education sector.
    • My role involves customer communication and project coordination.
    • I have a background in design and digital marketing.
    • My main area of expertise is language training.

    Nuance and register

    • job = common, everyday
    • role = more professional
    • position = often formal / HR-like
    • career = long-term professional path
    • background = education + experience + profile

    Upgrade lab

    Basic: I am a teacher.
    More natural: I work as a teacher.
    More professional: I teach English to adult learners.
    More developed: I work as an English teacher, mainly with adult learners and professionals.

    2. Family, Relationships, and Life Stages

    Core vocabulary

    • family
    • parents
    • mother
    • father
    • mum
    • dad
    • brother
    • sister
    • husband
    • wife
    • partner
    • boyfriend
    • girlfriend
    • son
    • daughter
    • child / children
    • relative
    • cousin
    • uncle
    • aunt
    • nephew
    • niece
    • single
    • married
    • divorced
    • engaged

    Useful combinations

    • have a brother / sister
    • get married
    • start a family
    • spend time with your family
    • be close to someone
    • get on well with someone
    • grow up in a small town
    • have children
    • raise children
    • support each other

    Typical sentence patterns

    • I have two sisters.
    • I'm married and I have one child.
    • We get on very well.
    • I grew up in a small village.
    • My parents live near me.

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • I have 30 years → I am 30
    • I am married with Anna → I am married to Anna
    • I have a son of 10 years → I have a ten-year-old son / My son is ten
    • We are in four in family → There are four people in my family
    • I am an only child is correct, but do not say unique son for figlio unico

    Mini examples

    • I have a younger brother and an older sister.
    • We don't live together, but we're very close.
    • She got married last year and now lives abroad.

    Professional upgrade

    Useful vocabulary for more mature descriptions: - sibling - household - upbringing - childhood - adolescence - adulthood - elderly relatives - caregiving - family responsibilities - work-life balance

    Higher-value expressions

    • I come from a close-knit family.
    • My upbringing had a strong influence on my values.
    • Balancing work and family responsibilities can be challenging.
    • I help take care of an elderly relative.

    Nuance and lexical precision

    • childhood = the period when you were a child
    • upbringing = the way you were raised
    • household = the people living in one home
    • relative = general family member
    • sibling = brother or sister

    Common translation traps

    • education is not always educazione.
      In family contexts, educazione is often better translated as:
    • manners
    • the way someone was raised
    • upbringing

    3. Home, Rooms, Furniture, and Everyday Objects

    Core vocabulary

    • house
    • flat / apartment
    • building
    • room
    • bedroom
    • bathroom
    • kitchen
    • living room
    • dining room
    • hallway
    • balcony
    • garden
    • floor
    • wall
    • door
    • window
    • table
    • chair
    • sofa
    • bed
    • lamp
    • shelf
    • wardrobe
    • desk
    • mirror
    • fridge
    • cooker / stove
    • washing machine
    • shower
    • towel
    • keys
    • bag
    • phone
    • charger

    Useful combinations

    • live in a flat
    • rent a house
    • move house
    • clean the kitchen
    • make the bed
    • do the washing
    • hang clothes
    • charge your phone
    • leave your keys at home
    • sit on the sofa
    • put something on the shelf
    • tidy your room

    Typical sentence patterns

    • There is a desk in my bedroom.
    • There are two bathrooms in the house.
    • I left my keys on the table.
    • My flat is small but comfortable.
    • We moved house last summer.

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • In my house there is... → In my house, there is... is possible, but often There is ... in my house sounds better
    • I have forgotten the keys at home → I left my keys at home
    • I have the phone in charge → My phone is charging / I'm charging my phone
    • I live in a home → I live in a house / flat
    • I do the bed → I make the bed

    Mini examples

    • My flat is on the third floor and it has a small balcony.
    • Could you put the bags in the kitchen?
    • I usually work at the desk in my room.

    Professional upgrade

    Vocabulary for more precise description: - spacious - cosy / cozy - bright - cluttered - tidy - messy - well-furnished - open-plan - storage space - neighbourhood / neighborhood - residential area - commute

    Useful higher-level combinations

    • a bright open-plan kitchen
    • a quiet residential area
    • limited storage space
    • a long commute to work
    • a well-connected neighbourhood

    Nuance and natural use

    • house = building / home type
    • home = emotional / personal concept
    • flat = British English
    • apartment = more common in American English
    • area is more neutral than zone in most everyday contexts

    Upgrade lab

    Basic: My house is big.
    Better: My flat is quite spacious.
    More natural: I live in a fairly spacious flat in a quiet area.
    More developed: I live in a fairly spacious flat in a quiet residential area, but the commute is quite long.

    4. Daily Routine, Time, and Frequency

    Core vocabulary

    • day
    • week
    • month
    • year
    • morning
    • afternoon
    • evening
    • night
    • weekday
    • weekend
    • today
    • tomorrow
    • yesterday
    • now
    • later
    • early
    • late
    • always
    • usually
    • often
    • sometimes
    • rarely
    • hardly ever
    • never
    • before
    • after
    • during
    • while

    Daily routine verbs

    • wake up
    • get up
    • get dressed
    • have breakfast
    • go to work
    • go to school
    • start work
    • finish work
    • have lunch
    • go home
    • cook dinner
    • relax
    • watch TV
    • go to bed
    • fall asleep

    Useful combinations

    • wake up early
    • be in a hurry
    • run late
    • on weekdays
    • at the weekend
    • from Monday to Friday
    • after work
    • before dinner
    • during the day
    • once a week
    • twice a month

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • I make breakfast → I make breakfast is possible if you prepare it, but for eating use have breakfast
    • I go to the bed → I go to bed
    • I do sport → I do sport / play sport depends on context, but in general English exercise is often more natural
    • At the morning → In the morning
    • At night vs in the evening: they are not interchangeable

    Mini examples

    • I usually wake up at 6:30 and go to work by bus.
    • On weekdays I don't have much free time.
    • I often study English in the evening.

    Professional upgrade

    Time and routine vocabulary for more developed speech: - routine - schedule - timetable - workload - shift - flexible hours - peak time - downtime - a packed day - a demanding schedule

    Natural higher-level combinations

    • stick to a routine
    • have a demanding schedule
    • fit something into your day
    • free up some time
    • manage your time better
    • have a fairly flexible schedule

    Nuance and precision

    • routine = repeated pattern
    • schedule = planned organization of time
    • timetable = especially transport / school / fixed schedule
    • workload = amount of work
    • downtime = time to relax / not work

    Upgrade lab

    Basic: I am very busy every day.
    Better: My days are usually quite busy.
    More natural: I have a fairly demanding schedule during the week.
    More professional: During the week I have a fairly demanding schedule, so I need to plan my time carefully.

    5. Food, Meals, and Eating Out

    Core vocabulary

    • food
    • meal
    • breakfast
    • lunch
    • dinner
    • snack
    • bread
    • rice
    • pasta
    • meat
    • fish
    • vegetables
    • fruit
    • cheese
    • milk
    • water
    • coffee
    • tea
    • juice
    • dessert
    • menu
    • bill
    • table
    • booking / reservation
    • waiter / waitress / server
    • dish
    • starter
    • main course
    • side dish

    Useful verbs

    • eat
    • drink
    • cook
    • prepare
    • order
    • book a table
    • pay the bill
    • have dinner
    • try a dish
    • share a starter
    • cut
    • boil
    • fry
    • bake

    Quantity language

    • a bottle of water
    • a glass of wine
    • a cup of tea
    • a slice of bread
    • a piece of cake
    • a bowl of soup
    • a plate of pasta

    Useful combinations

    • have lunch at work
    • cook from scratch
    • eat out
    • grab a quick bite
    • skip breakfast
    • order takeaway / takeout
    • pay separately
    • split the bill

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • I take a coffee → I have a coffee / I'll have a coffee
    • I like very much pasta → I like pasta very much
    • I want water is grammatically possible, but I'd like some water is more polite
    • a bread → some bread / a loaf of bread / a piece of bread
    • I am agree with this dish → I like this dish / I'm happy with this dish

    Mini examples

    • I'd like a table for two, please.
    • Could we see the menu?
    • I usually have a light lunch and a bigger dinner.
    • We decided to split the bill.

    Professional upgrade

    More developed vocabulary: - ingredients - flavour / flavor - spicy - mild - filling - homemade - processed food - balanced diet - eating habits - dietary requirements - vegetarian / vegan / gluten-free

    Higher-level combinations

    • develop healthy eating habits
    • follow a balanced diet
    • be high in sugar
    • be low in fat
    • cater for dietary requirements
    • eat on the go
    • make healthier choices

    Nuance and lexical precision

    • dish = prepared food item
    • meal = breakfast / lunch / dinner event
    • course = part of a formal meal
    • flavour = taste quality
    • ingredient = what the food contains

    Translation traps

    • sensible in food or health contexts usually means reasonable, not sensibile
    • eventually does not mean eventualmente
    • actually does not mean attualmente

    Core vocabulary

    • shop / store
    • supermarket
    • market
    • pharmacy
    • clothes shop
    • shoe shop
    • online shop
    • price
    • cost
    • money
    • cash
    • card
    • receipt
    • discount
    • sale
    • size
    • colour
    • brand
    • customer
    • shop assistant
    • change
    • wallet
    • order
    • delivery
    • return
    • refund

    Useful verbs

    • buy
    • pay
    • spend
    • save
    • order
    • return
    • exchange
    • try on
    • fit
    • cost
    • afford
    • compare prices
    • look for

    Useful combinations

    • pay by card
    • pay in cash
    • get a refund
    • ask for a receipt
    • try something on
    • be on sale
    • be out of stock
    • place an order
    • free delivery
    • compare products
    • save money

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • How much costs it? → How much does it cost?
    • I pay with card → I pay by card / with a card
    • Can I change this? may work, but in shops Can I exchange this? is often better
    • I search a gift → I am looking for a gift
    • This shirt wears well is not the normal way to say it fits you → This shirt fits me well

    Mini examples

    • How much does this cost?
    • Do you have this in a larger size?
    • I'd like to return this because it doesn't fit.
    • We usually buy groceries online.

    Professional upgrade

    Extended vocabulary: - budget - affordable - overpriced - bargain - value for money - impulse purchase - subscription - purchase history - customer support - delivery window - return policy

    Useful combinations

    • stay within budget
    • offer good value for money
    • make an impulse purchase
    • contact customer support
    • check the return policy
    • track your order
    • miss the delivery window

    Nuance and register

    • cheap can sound negative
    • inexpensive / affordable are often safer
    • expensive = high price
    • overpriced = too expensive for what it is worth
    • good value = worth the money

    Upgrade lab

    Basic: It is cheap.
    Better: It's quite affordable.
    More natural: It's reasonably priced and good value for money.
    More developed: It's reasonably priced, good value for money, and probably a better option than the more expensive brands.

    • neighbourhood / neighborhood
    • centre / center
    • station
    • bus stop
    • post office
    • bank
    • hospital
    • clinic
    • pharmacy
    • school
    • university
    • office
    • park
    • museum
    • cinema
    • gym
    • café / cafe
    • restaurant
    • library
    • police station

    Useful location language

    • near
    • far from
    • next to
    • opposite
    • between
    • behind
    • in front of
    • on the left
    • on the right
    • at the end of the street
    • around the corner

    Useful combinations

    • take the bus
    • catch a train
    • miss a stop
    • go straight on
    • turn left
    • cross the road
    • ask for directions
    • get lost
    • be close to the station
    • live in the city centre
    • use local services

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • I go in the city centre → I go to the city centre
    • Turn at left → Turn left
    • Go straight ahead is correct, but often Go straight on is more common in directions
    • I take the metro at this station is possible, but from this station is often what you mean
    • The pharmacy is front of the bank → The pharmacy is in front of the bank

    Mini examples

    • Is there a pharmacy near here?
    • Go straight on and turn right at the traffic lights.
    • I live in a quiet area, not far from the centre.

    Professional upgrade

    More developed town and service vocabulary: - facilities - amenities - public transport - commuter line - pedestrian area - residential district - business district - accessible - well-connected - overcrowded - reliable service

    Useful combinations

    • have access to good public transport
    • live in a well-connected area
    • local amenities are within walking distance
    • rely on public services
    • commute into the city centre
    • an overcrowded urban area

    Nuance and precision

    • area = neutral
    • district = more formal / administrative
    • suburb = area outside city centre, often residential
    • facilities = what a place offers
    • amenities = useful nearby services and conveniences

    • journey = movement from A to B
    • holiday / vacation = time off
    • commute = regular travel to work / study

    Useful verbs and combinations

    • feel ill
    • have a headache
    • catch a cold
    • take medicine
    • make an appointment
    • see a doctor
    • recover from an illness
    • stay in bed
    • have a temperature
    • feel better
    • get worse
    • get some rest
    • be in pain

    Common mistakes for Italian speakers

    • I have pain to the head → I have a headache / I have pain in my head
    • I am sickened → I feel sick / I am ill
    • I have 38 of fever → I have a temperature / I have a fever
    • I take care to my health → I take care of my health
    • I am allergic at → I am allergic to

    Mini examples

    • I haven't been feeling well lately.
    • You should make an appointment with your doctor.
    • I stayed at home because I had a sore throat and a temperature.

    Professional upgrade

    Extended health vocabulary: - wellbeing / well-being - mental health - stress-related - fatigue - symptoms - diagnosis - treatment - recovery - lifestyle - healthy habits - work-related stress

    Useful combinations

    • experience stress-related symptoms
    • recover fully
    • follow a treatment plan
    • develop healthier habits
    • manage stress more effectively

    Nuance and lexical precision

    • ill = more formal / British
    • sick = common, especially American English
    • pain = general sensation
    • ache = continuous pain in a body part
    • disease / illness / condition are not always interchangeable

    Translation traps

    • sensible ≠ sensibile
    • eventual ≠ eventuale
    • constipated ≠ raffreddato; it means stitico
