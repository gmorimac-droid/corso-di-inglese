    Unità 39 – Advanced Speaking (Storytelling & Arguments)

    Obiettivo della lezione

    In questa unità porti il tuo speaking a un livello più maturo lavorando su:

    • story structure;
    • narrative pacing;
    • argument building;
    • uso strategico di linkers, comment clauses e stance markers;
    • gestione di esempi, precisazioni e conclusioni;
    • produzione orale più lunga, più densa e più convincente.

    L’obiettivo non è solo parlare di più, ma parlare con architettura.

    Perché storytelling e argomentazione contano davvero

    A livello B2, una buona performance orale non dipende solo dal vocabolario o dalla grammatica. Dipende molto anche da:

    • come organizzi le idee;
    • quanto bene segnali il passaggio da una fase all’altra;
    • come introduci dettagli, spiegazioni e conseguenze;
    • quanto riesci a sostenere un’opinione senza sembrare schematico.

    Molti studenti riescono a dire frasi corrette, ma fanno fatica a:

    • costruire una narrativa coerente;
    • evitare salti logici;
    • passare dai fatti all’interpretazione;
    • sostenere una posizione con esempi convincenti.

    È qui che lo speaking comincia a sembrare davvero B2.

    La struttura di una buona storia orale

    Un racconto efficace di solito contiene cinque elementi:

    1. Context – situazione iniziale;
    2. Trigger / turning point – cosa fa partire la storia;
    3. Development – cosa succede dopo;
    4. Outcome – come si conclude;
    5. Reflection – cosa significa per te o cosa hai imparato.

    Formula utile

    What happened was that...

    At first...

    Then things changed when...

    In the end...

    Looking back, I think...

    Questa struttura ti permette di evitare liste di eventi senza direzione.

    Narrative progression: come far avanzare il discorso

    Una storia forte non è solo una sequenza di fatti. Deve anche dare al listener la sensazione che il racconto stia andando da qualche parte.

    Linkers narrativi utili

    Sequencing

    • at first
    • to begin with
    • then
    • shortly afterwards
    • meanwhile
    • eventually
    • in the end

    Turning points

    • the problem started when...
    • that was the moment when...
    • what changed everything was...
    • from that point on...

    Reflection

    • what I realised was that...
    • looking back, ...
    • in hindsight, ...
    • the main lesson for me was...

    Densità informativa: dalla frase scolastica alla frase naturale

    Confronta:

    I went to an event. It was interesting. I met people. It was useful.

    Versione più matura:

    I went to a professional event last month, and it turned out to be much more useful than I had expected because I met several people working in my field and ended up having two really valuable conversations.

    Perché funziona meglio:

    • integra più informazioni in una sola unità;
    • segnala relazione tra i fatti;
    • introduce valutazione personale;
    • suona meno scolastica e più naturale.

    Argomentare in modo credibile

    Una buona argomentazione orale di livello B2 spesso segue questo schema:

    1. claim – la tua tesi;
    2. reason – perché la sostieni;
    3. example – un caso concreto;
    4. comment / implication – perché quell’esempio conta.

    Modello base

    I believe that...

    The main reason is that...

    For example...

    This shows that...

    Esempio

    I believe that working abroad can be extremely valuable. The main reason is that it pushes people to adapt quickly and communicate more effectively. For example, when a friend of mine moved to the Netherlands, she improved not only her English but also her problem-solving skills. This shows that international experience often has a wider impact than people expect.

    Stance markers e hedging

    Per parlare in modo maturo non devi sempre fare affermazioni assolute. Spesso è meglio modulare ciò che dici.

    Stance markers

    • I think...
    • I believe...
    • Personally, ...
    • From my point of view, ...
    • As far as I’m concerned, ...

    Hedging

    • to some extent
    • in many cases
    • generally speaking
    • it seems to me that...
    • I wouldn’t say that..., but...
    • that depends on the situation

    Questo ti aiuta a sembrare più preciso e meno rigido.

    Costruire discorsi più lunghi senza perdersi

    Quando devi parlare per 2–4 minuti, usa dei blocchi mentali chiari.

    Formula 1: story block

    • where / when
    • what happened
    • why it mattered
    • how it ended
    • what you learned

    Formula 2: opinion block

    • opinion
    • reason 1
    • example
    • reason 2
    • short conclusion

    Formula 3: compare and evaluate

    • introduce two options
    • say which is better and why
    • mention limits / exceptions
    • conclude

    Example answer: storytelling

    A few months ago, I attended a workshop that I originally thought would be quite boring. At first, I only went because a colleague recommended it, and I didn’t really expect much from it. However, once the session started, I realised it was much more practical than I had imagined. The speaker used real examples, and that made the content easier to follow. What made the biggest difference, though, was the discussion part, because participants were encouraged to share their own experiences. In the end, I came away with a few useful ideas that I later applied to my own work. Looking back, I’d say the main lesson was that even events that seem unpromising can turn out to be surprisingly valuable.

    Example answer: argument

    Personally, I think travelling is one of the most effective ways to learn. The main reason is that it exposes people to real communication rather than controlled classroom situations. For instance, when you are in another country, you often have to solve small problems, ask questions, and adapt quickly to unfamiliar situations. That kind of experience tends to build confidence very fast. That said, I wouldn’t claim that travelling is the only way to learn, because a lot also depends on motivation and attitude. Still, in many cases, it gives learners a kind of practical pressure that can be extremely useful.

    Common speaking problems for Italian speakers

    1. Listing instead of structuring

    ❌ idea 1 + idea 2 + idea 3 without hierarchy

    Meglio:

    ✅ signal the main point, then support it.

    2. Overusing “and”

    ❌ I went there and it was nice and I met people and it was useful and I liked it.

    Meglio usare varietà:

    • however
    • as a result
    • in the end
    • what I found most interesting was...
    • the reason why...

    3. Opinion without evidence

    ❌ I think it is important.

    ✅ I think it is important because..., for example..., so...

    4. Finishing too early

    Molti studenti danno una risposta corretta ma troppo breve.

    Per allungarla in modo naturale, aggiungi:

    • a concrete example;
    • a contrast;
    • a consequence;
    • a personal reflection.

    Fluency extensions: formule molto utili

    • What made it interesting was...
    • The thing I remember most clearly is...
    • What happened next was that...
    • The main reason I say that is...
    • That’s one of the reasons why...
    • If I had to sum it up, I’d say...
    • What I learned from that experience was...

    Speaking drills

    Drill 1: expand the sentence

    Espandi oralmente:

    1. I went to an event.
    2. It was useful.
    3. I think online learning is effective.
    4. The experience changed my mind.

    Drill 2: story skeleton

    Parla seguendo questa traccia:

    • where were you?
    • why were you there?
    • what happened?
    • what changed?
    • how did it end?
    • what did you learn?

    Drill 3: opinion with support

    Rispondi per 90–120 secondi:

    1. Do people learn more from books or from experience?
    2. Is remote work better than office work?
    3. Are short courses really useful?

    Obbligo:

    • one clear claim;
    • at least two supporting reasons;
    • one example;
    • one balanced remark.

    Transformation lab

    Rendi queste risposte più mature e strutturate.

    1. I went to a meeting. It was good.
    2. I think travel is important because yes.
    3. It was difficult but good.
    4. I learned a lot.

    Possibili upgrade

    1. I went to a meeting last week, and it turned out to be more useful than I expected because the discussion was focused and practical.
    2. I think travel is important because it forces people to deal with real situations and become more independent.
    3. Although it was quite challenging at first, it ended up being a positive experience overall.
    4. I learned a lot, especially about how important preparation and communication are in unfamiliar situations.

    Writing task

    Task 1 – narrative paragraph

    Scrivi un paragrafo di 140–170 parole su un’esperienza che ti ha insegnato qualcosa.

    Deve includere:

    • contesto;
    • evento principale;
    • difficoltà o sorpresa;
    • risultato;
    • riflessione finale.

    Task 2 – opinion paragraph

    Scrivi un testo di 140–180 parole su questo tema:

    Do people develop more through challenging experiences than through routine?

    Deve includere:

    • una posizione chiara;
    • almeno due ragioni;
    • un esempio;
    • una mini-conclusione.

    Checklist di revisione

    Controlla se la tua produzione orale o scritta:

    • ha una struttura visibile;
    • usa linkers vari e non solo and;
    • distingue fatti, opinioni e conseguenze;
    • contiene almeno un esempio concreto;
    • si chiude con una conclusione o una riflessione;
    • suona naturale e non frammentata.

    Self-check finale

    Sai:

    • raccontare un’esperienza con una struttura chiara?
    • sostenere un’opinione con ragioni ed esempi?
    • usare stance markers e hedging in modo naturale?
    • rendere una risposta più lunga senza riempirla di ripetizioni?
    • passare da speaking “scolastico” a speaking più convincente?

    Se sì, stai costruendo una performance orale molto più vicina a un vero B2 high-performance speaking.
